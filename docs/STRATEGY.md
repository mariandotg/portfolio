# Landing Page Strategy — Mariano Guillaume

## What we're building and why

### The problem with the old page
The original landing page was a developer portfolio: "I build web products that drive business results." Generic, centered on skills and projects. It talked about *me*, not about *the client's problem*.

A portfolio attracts other developers. A sales page attracts clients with budget.

The redesign flips the frame: every section now speaks to what the client needs, fears, and wants — not what I can do technically.

---

## Target audience (priority order)

**1. Business clients (primary)**
Small to mid-sized businesses that need a web product built — e-commerce stores, internal tools, customer-facing apps, platforms. These clients have a budget and a business problem, not a technical one. They don't care about the tech stack until you've earned their trust.

**2. Contractor / SWE roles (secondary)**
CTOs and engineering managers looking for a reliable freelance dev to embed in their team. This audience is less urgent right now but can be captured via the blog and LinkedIn presence.

**The page is optimized for audience #1.** Audience #2 can use the same conversion flow — "Book a Call" is appropriate for both.

---

## Conversion structure

The landing page follows a proven sales page structure:

```
Problem awareness → Solution clarity → Credibility → Process trust → Offer → CTA
```

### Section breakdown

**Hero**
- "Available for new projects" badge — signals legitimacy and immediacy
- Headline: outcome-first ("grow your business"), not tech-first
- Subhead: clarifies WHO it's for and WHAT the promise is
- Primary CTA: "Book a Free Call" — low commitment, high intent
- Secondary CTA: "How I Work" — scrolls to process for skeptics
- Tech trust bar: establishes credibility without bragging

**Pain Points — "Sound familiar?"**
Empathy before pitch. Three pain points that mirror real objections business owners have when hiring freelancers:
1. Unreliable freelancers → I'm reliable and proactive
2. Messy code → I write clean, scalable architecture
3. No support after launch → I'm a long-term partner

This section does the selling before the services section. It shows you understand the client.

**Services — "What I Build"**
Four cards, outcome-framed (not tech-framed):
1. Web Applications & MVPs
2. E-commerce & Platforms ← soft mention of white-label/multi-tenant play
3. Technical Strategy
4. Ongoing Development

The 2×2 grid (vs 3-col) gives each service room to breathe and feel substantial.

**Process — "How I Work"**
This is the trust anchor when you don't have case studies yet. A structured process signals professionalism. Four steps: Discovery → Proposal → Build → Deliver.

**Credibility — "Built on reliability"**
Stats (5+ years, global, end-to-end, on-time) + tech stack badges. This replaces portfolio work while the portfolio is being built. Once real case studies exist, swap this section out for a featured work grid.

**Blog**
Conditional — only shows if posts exist. Builds authority and SEO over time.

**CTA Banner**
Strong close: "Ready to build your next product?" + "Book a free 30-minute discovery call. No commitment — let's see if we're the right fit."

## The SaaS white-label play

### What it is
Build a reusable e-commerce/platform codebase that can be deployed per client with minor customization (branding, domain, content, features). Think Shopify-lite for niche industries or multi-tenant platforms for specific verticals.

### Why it makes sense
- Same work, sold multiple times
- Customization layer becomes a paid service
- Support + maintenance = recurring revenue
- Scales into an agency model

### Current approach (phase 1)
The landing page **soft-mentions** this via the "E-commerce & Platforms" service card:
> "Online stores, marketplaces, and multi-tenant platforms customized to your brand and business model."

This is intentional. Don't lead with the SaaS angle until you have a working prototype to show.

### When to promote it (phase 2)
Once you have one deployed client on the platform:
1. Add a dedicated "Solutions" section to the landing page
2. Create a case study showing the deployment + customization
3. Target specific verticals (restaurants, gyms, real estate agencies, clinics) with tailored landing pages or outreach

## Agency evolution roadmap

### Phase 1 — Solo freelancer (now)
- Land 2-3 business clients via this landing page
- Build case studies from real projects
- Establish the process and positioning
- Start building the white-label codebase on the side

### Phase 2 — Productized services
- Package services with fixed scopes and prices (e.g., "E-commerce Starter: $5k, 4 weeks")
- Launch the white-label platform with 1-2 clients
- Add a "Solutions" page alongside "Services"
- Build an email list through the blog

### Phase 3 — Studio / micro-agency
- Bring in a designer and a second developer
- Shift personal brand from "Mariano the freelancer" to "studio" (keep personal site as entry point)
- The white-label platform becomes the agency's flagship product
- Recurring revenue from support contracts funds hiring

### Phase 4 — Agency
- Team of 4-8 (design, dev, PM, sales)
- The landing page evolves into an agency homepage
- Personal brand becomes thought leadership (blog, speaking, content)

## Content principles

- **Client-first language:** "Your business needs X" not "I offer X"
- **Outcome over features:** "Launch fast" not "uses Next.js"
- **Empathy before pitch:** Acknowledge the client's pain before selling the solution
- **Process as proof:** Show how you work before they ask — it pre-handles the "can I trust you?" objection
- **Low commitment CTA:** "Book a Free Call" has lower friction than "Hire Me" or "Get a Quote"
