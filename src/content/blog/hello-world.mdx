Most projects do not fail because of code quality.  
They fail because scope, decision ownership, and success criteria are fuzzy at kickoff.

When I work with a new client, I use a five-day scoping process to reduce delivery risk before implementation starts.

## Day 1: Business Context

I map the problem to measurable business impact:

- What process is broken today?
- What is the cost of not fixing it?
- Who owns the outcome on the client side?

Output: a one-page problem statement with explicit success metrics.

## Day 2: User Flows and Constraints

I define the critical user journeys and non-negotiable constraints:

- Core paths users must complete
- Compliance or security constraints
- Existing system dependencies

Output: annotated flow diagram and dependency map.

## Day 3: Scope Boundaries

I separate the first release into:

- Must-have for launch
- Should-have for next phase
- Nice-to-have backlog

Output: versioned scope document with explicit "not in v1" list.

## Day 4: Delivery Plan

I break the work into milestones with acceptance criteria:

- Milestone owner
- Delivery date
- Demo checklist

Output: timeline and milestone plan with weekly checkpoints.

## Day 5: Budget and Risk Review

I align budget and timeline against the scoped milestones, then call out the top delivery risks and mitigation strategy.

Output: final proposal with transparent assumptions.

## Why This Works

A structured scoping week reduces ambiguity before development, shortens decision cycles, and gives both sides a clear operating model for delivery.
