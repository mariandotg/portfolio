I left my last full-time role in January. Not dramatically — no burnout, no layoff, no startup collapse. Just a deliberate decision to go solo for a while and see what that felt like.

Three months in, here's what I didn't expect.

## The feedback loop is brutal

In a team, bad decisions have a long half-life. You make a poor architecture choice in Q1 and the real consequences show up in Q3, diffused across three engineers who've half-forgotten the context. You rarely get clean causality.

Solo contract work collapses that. I shipped a broken migration script two weeks in and spent an entire weekend fixing it — alone, no one to share the blame, no one to share the work. That specific pain is now a permanent part of how I think about migration scripts.

I'm not sure teams are less careful, exactly. But the feedback is noisier. Solo work is uncomfortably direct.

## Scope is the hardest skill

The thing no one tells you about contracting is that the technical work is rarely the hard part. The hard part is agreeing on what "done" means before you start.

I've gotten burned twice by implicit scope. Once on a backend API where the client assumed I was also handling the auth layer (I wasn't, until I was). Once on a "small refactor" that turned into a full service extraction because the codebase had more coupling than the brief implied.

Both times the work itself was fine. The communication leading up to it wasn't. I've since added a scope-confirmation step to every engagement — a short written summary of exactly what I'm delivering, what I'm not delivering, and what happens if we find something unexpected. Clients appreciate it. It's saved me hours.

## Rate anxiety is real and mostly irrational

I spent a month second-guessing my rates. Too high and I'd lose the engagement. Too low and I'd resent the work. I found a number I was comfortable with, and then immediately wondered if I'd left money on the table.

The rational answer is: your rate is what the market pays you. If a client accepts it, it was at most slightly too low, not drastically. If they negotiate, you learn the ceiling.

What I've noticed is that rate anxiety is usually about something else — a fear that you're not actually worth it, that someone will look at your work and find it lacking. That's an impostor syndrome problem, not a pricing problem. Solving it requires doing the work and watching it land, not adjusting the number.

## What I'm building toward

The honest answer is that I'm still figuring it out. The blog is partly for me — a way to think through things by writing them down. The contracting is funding the space to do that thinking.

If you're reading this and considering a similar move: the safety net you're looking for is mostly a fiction anyway. The real one is skills and reputation, and those you already have.
